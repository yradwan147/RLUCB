\section{Methodology}
\label{sec:method}

We describe the simulation framework used to evaluate adaptive question selection.
The framework consists of three components: a student model with learning and forgetting dynamics (\cref{sec:student_model}), two question selection strategies (\cref{sec:selection}), and the experimental design (\cref{sec:exp_design}).

\subsection{Student Model}
\label{sec:student_model}
\vspace{-1mm}

Each simulated student maintains a \textbf{knowledge vector} $\mathbf{k} = [k_1, k_2, \ldots, k_C]$, where $k_i \in [0, 1]$ represents the probability of answering correctly in category $i$ and $C = 6$ is the number of knowledge categories.
Initial knowledge is sampled from a truncated normal distribution:
\begin{equation}
    k_i^{(0)} \sim \text{clip}\Big(\mathcal{N}(\mu \cdot d_i,\; \sigma^2),\; 0.05,\; 0.95\Big),
    \label{eq:init_knowledge}
\end{equation}
where $\mu = 0.35$ is the mean knowledge level, $d_i \in [0.3, 0.7]$ is the difficulty of category $i$ (sampled uniformly), and $\sigma = 0.1$.
When a student is quizzed on category $i$, they answer correctly with probability $k_i$ (Bernoulli trial).

\noindent\textbf{Learning Dynamics.}
After answering a question in category $i$, knowledge is updated asymptotically:
\begin{equation}
    k_i \leftarrow
    \begin{cases}
        k_i + \alpha \cdot (1 - k_i), & \text{if correct (asymptotic gain toward 1.0)}, \\
        k_i - \beta \cdot k_i, & \text{if incorrect (multiplicative penalty)},
    \end{cases}
    \label{eq:learning}
\end{equation}
where $\alpha = 0.12$ is the learning rate and $\beta = 0.02$ is the incorrect-answer penalty.
The asymptotic form ensures diminishing returns as knowledge approaches mastery, a well-established property of skill acquisition~\cite{anderson2014engaged}.

\noindent\textbf{Forgetting Dynamics.}
At each timestep, all categories \emph{not} exposed in that step undergo Ebbinghaus-inspired exponential decay~\cite{ebbinghaus1885memory,murre2015replication}:
\begin{equation}
    k_i \leftarrow k_{\text{base}} + (k_i - k_{\text{base}}) \cdot e^{-\lambda},
    \label{eq:forgetting}
\end{equation}
where $k_{\text{base}} = 0.10$ is the retention floor (minimum knowledge that persists) and $\lambda = 0.01$ is the decay rate per timestep.
This model captures the key property that knowledge decays toward a baseline over time without reinforcement, with the decay rate compounding naturally over consecutive unexposed timesteps.

\subsection{Question Selection Strategies}
\label{sec:selection}
\vspace{-1mm}

We compare two selection strategies in a between-subjects simulation design:

\noindent\textbf{UCB Selection (Adaptive).}
The next category to quiz is selected by maximizing the UCB1 score:
\begin{equation}
    \text{UCB}(i) = \underbrace{(1 - r_i)}_{\text{exploitation}} + \underbrace{c \cdot \sqrt{\frac{\ln N}{n_i}}}_{\text{exploration}},
    \label{eq:ucb}
\end{equation}
where $r_i$ is the cumulative correctness rate for category $i$, $N$ is the total number of questions asked, $n_i$ is the number of attempts on category $i$, and $c = \sqrt{2} \approx 1.414$ is the exploration parameter.
The exploitation term $(1 - r_i)$ inverts the standard reward maximization: categories with \emph{lower} correctness rates receive \emph{higher} priority, directing practice toward weak areas.
The exploration term encourages probing under-tested categories, ensuring no topic is neglected.
Any category with $n_i = 0$ is selected first (round-robin initialization).

\noindent\textbf{Random Selection (Control).}
Categories are selected uniformly at random with probability $\nicefrac{1}{C}$ each.
This serves as a strong baseline representing the default behavior of most quiz platforms.

\subsection{Experimental Design}
\label{sec:exp_design}
\vspace{-1mm}

The experiment simulates two groups of students---UCB and Control (Random)---under identical initial conditions to isolate the effect of the selection strategy.
Both groups share the same initial knowledge distributions and random seeds per student, differing only in question selection.
Each group contains \textbf{100} students who each answer \textbf{10,000} questions across \textbf{6} knowledge categories.
The complete parameter set is given in \cref{tab:config}.

Each student has an individual UCB selector (or random selector), maintaining per-student statistics.
At each timestep, the selector chooses a category, the student answers (Bernoulli trial based on current knowledge), knowledge is updated via \cref{eq:learning}, the selector state is updated, and forgetting is applied to all unexposed categories via \cref{eq:forgetting}.

Group-level metrics are recorded at every timestep: mean knowledge across students, per-category knowledge, weakest-category knowledge (minimum across categories per student, averaged across students), knowledge variance, cumulative accuracy, and exposure distribution.
The experiment is replicated across 3 independent runs with different random seeds to verify robustness.
All results are analyzed across three temporal phases: \textbf{short-term} ($t \leq 500$), \textbf{mid-term} ($500 < t \leq 2{,}000$), and \textbf{long-term} ($t > 2{,}000$).

The simulation is implemented in Python using NumPy~\cite{harris2020array} for numerical computation and Gymnasium~\cite{towers2024gymnasium} as the RL environment interface.
The environment exposes the student's knowledge vector as the observation space and the category index as the action space, enabling future integration with standard RL algorithms.
