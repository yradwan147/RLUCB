\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

We presented \ours, an adaptive question selection strategy that applies the UCB1 algorithm to prioritize knowledge categories where students struggle most.
Through controlled simulations with 100 students per group over 10,000 questions, we demonstrated that \ours provides up to $60.7\%$ better performance in students' weakest knowledge areas during the critical early learning phase and achieves competitive overall knowledge in approximately $3{\times}$ fewer questions than random selection.

Our phase analysis reveals a nuanced picture: \ours excels in time-constrained settings ($< 1{,}000$ questions), where its exploration-exploitation trade-off produces dramatically faster and more balanced learning.
However, given unlimited practice, random selection converges to comparable or slightly higher knowledge levels, confirming that UCB's primary value is \emph{learning efficiency} rather than raising the ultimate knowledge ceiling.
The diminishing returns of adaptivity as categories receive sufficient practice suggest that hybrid strategies---UCB for early learning, random or spaced repetition for maintenance---may offer the best of both worlds.

This study has several limitations.
Our student model, while incorporating realistic learning and forgetting dynamics, is a simplification of real human cognition; validation with actual student data is needed before deployment.
The simulation assumes stationary category difficulties, whereas real learning involves evolving prerequisite relationships.
Additionally, the current framework evaluates only two selection strategies; future work should compare against Thompson Sampling, $\varepsilon$-greedy, and contextual bandit approaches that incorporate the student's knowledge state.
