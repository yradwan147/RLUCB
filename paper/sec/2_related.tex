\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related}
\vspace{-1mm}

\paragraph{Multi-Armed Bandits in Education.}
The multi-armed bandit (MAB) framework has been applied to various educational settings.
Clement et al.~\cite{clement2015multi} proposed using bandits for pedagogical sequence optimization, treating each learning activity as an arm.
Liu and Koedinger~\cite{liu2014trading} explored the exploration-exploitation trade-off in the context of practice scheduling.
Thompson Sampling has been used for A/B testing of instructional strategies~\cite{rafferty2019statistical}, and contextual bandits have been applied to content recommendation in MOOCs~\cite{shaikh2017adaptive}.
However, most prior work treats the selection problem at the level of individual items or activities rather than knowledge categories, and few explicitly model the temporal dynamics of learning and forgetting.
In contrast, \ours operates at the category level with explicit Ebbinghaus-inspired forgetting, providing a principled yet interpretable approach to adaptive selection.

\paragraph{Intelligent Tutoring Systems.}
Intelligent tutoring systems (ITS) have a long history of adapting instruction to individual learners~\cite{anderson1985intelligent,vanlehn2006behavior,vanlehn2011relative}.
Cognitive tutors based on ACT-R~\cite{anderson2014engaged} use knowledge tracing to model student mastery and select problems accordingly.
Bayesian Knowledge Tracing (BKT)~\cite{corbett1994knowledge} and its extensions~\cite{yudelson2013individualized} estimate per-skill mastery probabilities and have been widely adopted in practice.
Deep Knowledge Tracing (DKT)~\cite{piech2015deep} applies recurrent neural networks to model student knowledge from interaction sequences.
While these systems provide sophisticated student models, their question selection policies are often heuristic (e.g., select the least-mastered skill) without the formal exploration-exploitation guarantees provided by bandit algorithms.
Our work complements knowledge tracing by providing a principled selection mechanism with known theoretical properties.

\paragraph{Spaced Repetition and Forgetting.}
The spacing effect---the finding that distributed practice leads to better retention than massed practice---is one of the most robust phenomena in learning science~\cite{ebbinghaus1885memory,cepeda2006distributed}.
Spaced repetition systems such as Leitner boxes~\cite{leitner1972so} and SuperMemo~\cite{wozniak1995two} schedule review based on estimated memory strength.
More recent work has developed computational models of forgetting, including the ACT-R memory model~\cite{pavlik2005practice} and neural network approaches~\cite{settles2016trainable}.
Murre and Dros~\cite{murre2015replication} replicated Ebbinghaus's original forgetting curve experiments, confirming the exponential decay model.
Our student model incorporates Ebbinghaus-inspired exponential forgetting with a retention floor, which interacts with the UCB selection policy to create a natural spaced repetition effect: categories that decay below a threshold are more likely to be selected by UCB's weakness-targeting mechanism.

\paragraph{Reinforcement Learning for Education.}
Beyond bandits, full reinforcement learning (RL) has been applied to educational sequencing.
Doroudi et al.~\cite{doroudi2019where} surveyed RL approaches for instructional policy optimization.
Gymnasium-based environments~\cite{towers2024gymnasium} have been developed for simulating educational interactions, enabling the application of standard RL algorithms.
While deep RL methods can learn complex policies, they require large amounts of interaction data and lack the interpretability of simpler approaches.
Our work bridges the gap between simple heuristics and complex RL by using UCB1---which has provably optimal regret bounds~\cite{auer2002finite,lattimore2020bandit}---in a Gymnasium-compatible environment, enabling future comparison with more complex RL policies.

\textbf{\ours} differentiates itself from prior work by combining three elements rarely found together: (1) a bandit-based selection policy with formal guarantees, (2) explicit modeling of both learning and forgetting dynamics, and (3) a comprehensive evaluation framework that analyzes performance across multiple temporal phases, revealing when adaptive selection provides the greatest benefit.
